Theme 1: Population and Settlement


REQUIRED CASE STUDIES:
  • A country which is over-populated
  • A country which is under-populated
  • A country with a high rate of natural population growth
  • A country with a low rate of population growth (or population decline)
  • An international migration
  • A country with a high dependent population
  • A densely populated country or area (at any scale from local to regional)
  • A sparsely populated country or area (at any scale from local to regional)
  • Settlement and service provision in an area || MISSING - SHUNYI TRIP
  • An urban area (including changing land use and urban sprawl) || MISSING - SHUNYI TRIP
  • A rapidly growing urban area in a developing country and migration to it


    • CASE STUDY: BANGLADESH

  • A country that is over-populated
  • A country with a high rate of natural growth
  • A densely populated country


    • Population Density: ~1298 per km2
    Population Growth Rate: +1.6%
    Birth rate: 21.14 per 1000
    Death rate: 5.61 per 1000

    Contraceptive prevalence: 61.2%
    Average age at first birth: 18.1

    Physicians density: 0.36 per 1000
    Hospital beds density: 0.6 per 1000

    Terrain:
    - Mostly flat alluvial plain
    - Hilly in SE

    Land Use:
    - Agricultural : 70.1%
    - Forest : 11.1%
    - Other : 18.8%

    Most populated:
    - Area around the Ganges river
    - 200-600mm of rainfall

    Unemployment: 8.7%


    CASE STUDY: AUSTRALIA

  • A sparsely populated country
  • A country that is underpopulated


    • Population Density: ~3 per km2
    Population Growth Rate: +1.03%
    Birth Rate: 12.1 per 1000
    Death Rate: 7.3 per 1000

    Contraceptive prevalence: 72.3%
    Average age at first birth: 30.5

    Physicians density: 3.37 per 1000
    Hospital beds density: 3.9 per 1000
    21 years of free education
    11.6 million employed in the workforce

    Terrain: mostly low plateau with deserts; fertile plain in SE

    Land Use: - Agricultural : 53.4%
    - Forest : 19.3%

    CASE STUDY: ITALY

  • A country with a high dependent population
  • A country with a low rate of population growth


    • 8 / 10 Italians < 30 y/o live with their parents
    Average age for moving out: 36
    Average wage for Italians 25-30 = half of their counter-parts in England
    40% that leave their parents’ home must return
    Workers to pensioners’ ratio = 0.7
    2008: 21% of Italy’s GNP spent on caring for people > 65 y/o

    Population Growth Rate: 0.23%
    Birth Rate: 8.7 per 1000
    Death Rate: 10.3 per 1000
    Percentage population >65: 23.7%
    Total Dependency Ratio: 56.5%

    Mother’s mean age at first birth: 30.3
    Life expectancy at birth: 82.2
    Total fertility rate: 1.43 per woman

    CASE STUDY: International Migration from Mexico -> USA

  • An international migration


    • Crime rates (push factor): - 81% of Mexicans describe crime as a problem in Mexico
    - Homicide rates: 10-14 per 100,000 in Mexico ; lower in USA
    - Last 5 years: 47,500 killed in drug-related crimes

    Unemployment (push factor): - 2.2 (2002) -> 5.37 (2010)
    - 47% live below the poverty line

    Standards of living (both push/pull factor):
    - Mexico: 1 doctor per 1800
    - USA: 1 doctor per 400 - Life expectancy in Mexico = 72; USA = 76 - Adult literacy rate: Mexico = 55%; USA = 99%

    CASE STUDY: INTERNAL MIGRATION IN CHINA

  • A rapidly growing urban area in a developing country and migration to it


    • In 2009, 11% of the total population of China were rural to urban migrants (145 million)

    44.4% of new-generation workers were employed in the manufacturing industry

    First migration rates of migrants:
    - Born 1980-90 is 21.1
    - Born after 1990 is 17.2
    - More and more young people are migrating

    Effects:
    - Increased supply of labour in urban areas
    - Increased income
    - Increased environmental population
    - Overpopulation
    - Increased crime & safety issues
    - Increased demand for resources

    CASE STUDY: ROCINHA, RIO DE JANEIRO, BRAZIL



  • A rapidly growing urban area in a developing country and migration to it


    • Densely populated: 200,000 people live there Narrow streets
    Most of the favela is on a steep hill with many trees surrounding

    High crime rates: November 2011, hundreds of police and military patrolled streets to track drug dealers

    Spread of diseases by Aedes mosquitos (dengue)
    Other diseases:
    - Rocinha – Tuberculosis: 380 cases per 1000
    - Rest of Brazil – Tuberculosis: 37.5 cases per 1000

    CASE STUDY: RUSSIA’S DAY OF CONCEPTION


  • A pro-natalist population policy


    • First adopted in 2006
    On 12th September, citizens get the day off work
    If a child is born on 12th June, couples win prizes

    Why?
    - Few young people to work/pay taxes
    - Low population slowing Russia’s population growth
    - Spike in emigration after 1991
    - Population falling 700,000 annually
    - 2007: 141.4 million citizens, estimated < 100 million by 2050 at current rates

    Effects:
    - Birth rate increased by 4.5% in 2007
    - 1,479,600 -< 1,610,000 births from 2006 to 2007
    - 2010: total fertility rate 1.54 (from 1.2 in 2006)

    CASE STUDY: CHINA’S ONE/TWO CHILD POLICY


    An anti-natalist population policy

    25th September 1980: Politburo of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) issued and open letter urging members of the party & youth league to only have one child.
    - Before then, in 1970s, the government encouraged delaying marriages, longer intervals between births, and fewer children.
    - Before the family planning policy was introduced in 1978, the total fertility rate was 5

    Effects:
    - Government claims the policy helped achieve 400 million fewer births in the past 30 years.
    - Female infanticide, child abandonment.
    - Human trafficking of young women
    - In some rural areas, male : female = 6 : 1
    - Little emperor syndrome: only children gain seemingly
    excessive amounts of attention from their parents and grandparents

    Consequences for people that disobeyed the policy:
    - Forced sterilization (usually metal IUD)
    - 10% salary cut
    - Loss of free public education + healthcare for family
    - Fines of 10,000RMB

    NOTE: modified to two child policy in April 2016 due to ageing population

    Theme 2: The natural environment

    REQUIRED CASE STUDIES:
  • An earthquake
  • A volcano
  • The opportunities presented by a river, the hazards associated with it and their management
  • The opportunities presented by an area of coastline, the hazards associated with it and their management
  • An area of tropical rainforest
  • An area of hot desert


    • CASE STUDY: THE GREAT HANSHIN EARTHQUAKE


  • An earthquake


    • 17th January 1995
    Magnitude: 6.9
    Ground moved 18cm horizontally, 12 cm vertically
    Plates: Philippines plate subducted by Eurasian plate at the Nojima fault

    Environmental Effects:
    - 300 fires
    - 716 reported aftershocks
    - Liquefaction of soil = subsidence of artificial islands (e.g. Rokkō Island)

    Social Effects:
    - 400,000 buildings irreparably damaged
    - 120/150 quays in the port of Kobe damaged
    - 70% of Osaka-Kobe railway tracks out of operation
    - Daikai Station on Kobe Rapid Railway line collapsed
    - Part of National Route 28 damaged

    Economic Effects:
    - Mitsubishi and Panasonic forced to close
    - $120 billion in damage
    - 2.5% of Japan’s GDP used for clean-up

    CASE STUDY: MOUNT ST HELENS


  • A volcano


    • Type: stratovolcano
    Erupted 18th May 1980
    Plates: Juan de Fuca subducted by North American plate

    Environmental Effects:
    - Removed 396m of the summit, leaving a massive crater
    - Contaminated Toutle River
    - Filled in Spirit Lake

    Social Effects:
    - 57 deaths due to asphyxiation, burns, trauma

    Economic Effects:
    - $449.80 million on restoring forestry
    - $363.90 million on clean-up
    - $969.80 million in total

    CASE STUDY: MISSISSIPPI RIVER


  • Opportunities presented by a river, the hazards associated with it, and their management


    • Opportunities:
    - Fertile soil for farming corn, rice, and sweet potato
    - Water for irrigation
    - Transport: trade routes, ports (economic opportunities)
    - Flat land for building houses, roads, and railways (e.g. New Orleans and Baton Rouge)
    - Source of fish – aquaculture (famous for shrimp)

    Hazards:
    - Farmland can be flooded
    - Stores of food could be damaged by floods
    - Foundations of homes unstable
    - Bridges needed = large investment of time and money needed (e.g. Horace Wilkinson Bridge)
    - Insurance companies may refuse to insure homes (e.g. in Morgan City)
    - Poor market value

    CASE STUDY: COASTAL MANAGEMENT IN HOLDERNESS


  • The opportunities presented by an area of coastline, the hazards associated with it, and their management


    • Location: NE of England

    One of the world’s most vulnerable coastlines: retreats at a rate of 1-2m per year
    - Strong prevailing winds = longshore drift = material moves south
    - Cliffs are made of a soft boulder clay = erodes quickly

    Threatens the village of Mappleton (approximately 50 properties)

    1991: coastal management scheme costing 2 million GBP introduced
    - Rock armour built along the base of the cliff
    - Two rock groynes

  • Mappleton and the cliffs no longer at risk from erosion

  • Rock groynes have stopped beach material from being moved South of Mappleton

    • CASE STUDY: AMAZON RAINFOREST


  • An area of tropical rainforest


    • >50% of remaining rainforest on Earth

    Cattle sector = 80% of deforestation
    - 1995: 70% of formerly forested land  cattle ranching
    - 91% of remaining land deforested since 1995 for cattle ranching since 1970
    Other = small-scale subsistence farming/mechanized cropland

    Slash and Burn is employed by native people
    - Small areas of land cleared, vegetation burned
    - Nutrients from ash
    - Soil = fertile for a few years
    - When depleted, move on to another part
    - Area regenerates
    - No lasting damage = sustainable

    Response:
    - Norwegian government donated $1 billion to Amazon fund in 2008
    - September 2015: president Dilma Rousseff told UN that Brazil had reduced rate of deforestation by 82%
    - Reforestation: aim to restore and reforest 120,000km2 by 2030
    - National parks established to protect forestry

    CASE STUDY: THE SAHEL REGION

  • An area of hot desert


    • (looks specifically at 2012 drought)

    Crop failure and mass famine
    - FAO: agricultural production dropped by 25% from 2010
    - Mauritania: 52% crop production drop
    - Chad: 50% crop production fall
    - 2012: grain harvest down by ~1.4 million tons
    - 15 million malnourished in West Africa and Sahel Region
    - Malnutrition exceeded threshold levels of 15%

    Economic productivity
    - Food prices increase
    - Fewer workers
    - Vicious cycle

    Response
    - February 2012: UN convened emergency meeting; appealed for $725 million, only 20% raised by March
    - March 2012: Oxfam launched $36.3 million emergency appeal
    - UNHCR appealed for $36.5 million to help the average of ~1500 arriving in Mauritania daily
    - Cloud seeding in Burkina Faso (which started in 1998)
    - AMETSOC monitors droughts
    - 2012: FAO Drylands Restoration Initiative

    CASE STUDY: THE GREAT BARRIER REEF


  • A coral reef (not explicitly stated as a required case study, but questions are common)


    • Formed 13,000 years ago, when the sea level = 61m

    Requirements:
    - Sunlight
    - No pollution
    - Temperature: 23-25˚C
    - Tropical areas

    World Heritage Area since 1981

    Employs 6% of Australia’s workforce 12% of Australia’s exports
    - Nearly 5 billion Australian dollars generated from tourism annually

    Covers an area of 344,400 km2 (2,900 individual reefs, 900 islands)

    Biodiversity:
    - 30 species of whales, dolphins and porpoises

    • CASE STUDY: NIKE


  • A transnational corporation and its global links


    • HQ: Beaverton, Oregon, USA
    CEO: Mark Parker

    Nike’s shoe/clothing production takes place at Tangerang (25km west of Jakarta, Indonesia)
    Average wage of a Nike factory worker in an LEDC: $1.25
    Lebron James’ lifetime endorsement deal is worth $500 million
    2014: brand alone worth $19 billion


    Employs 44,000 people worldwide
    More than 700 shops open around the world
    Offices located in 45 countries outside USA

    As of July 2011, 2/3 of factories producing converse products did not meet worker treatment standards

    April 2014: strike in China factory:
    - 70,000 employees
    - Underpay of employees by 250RMB per month (for 20 years)
    - Average salary at YueYuen factory is 3000RMB

    CASE STUDY: WAYAN TOPELE’S BALINESE RICE FARM


  • A farm or agricultural system


    • Location: 7.5km N of the town of Ubud, near River Sala
    Area: 0.6 hectares – contiguous farm

    Operated by: family & contract laborers (when needed)
    Primarily a subsistence farm

    Fairly gentle gradient = only 2 levels needed
    - Section 1 – upper level – mix of IR36 and Padi Bali
    - Section 2 – lower level – IR36 + vegetable + grass near river

    Padi Bali = 6 months to cultivate; IR36 = 3 months to cultivate

    Harvested rice = 1/3 to government, 1/3 eaten, 1/3 barter for machinery

    Little/no education = no accurate records of production Normal year= 120kg of rice produced
    - 1994: tumero infestation = 50% of normal harvest
    - Harvest handed to government, destroyed, and free pesticide was provided

    Subak (irrigation system) gives him 2 quotas of water

    Very fertile soil:
    - Alluvial deposits from River Sala = underlayers fertile
    - 1963 eruption of Gunung Agung = surface layers fertile

    Pests: black moths, grasshoppers, Sangit mosquitos, birds

    Technology: high-yielding varieties of rice (IR36), fertilisers (e.g. TSP, urea, potassium chloride)

    CASE STUDY: AFGHATNISTAN’S FOOD SHORTAGE


  • A country or region suffering from food shortages


    • Location: Asia/Middle East
    Religion: Muslim, Taliban Extremists

    War: 30 years and counting (with Russia, Taliban, USA)

    Life expectancy at birth: 51.3 years (lowest in the world)
    Infant mortality rate: 112.8/1000 live births
    Child labour percentage: 25.3%
    Unemployment rate: 35% (2008)
    Population below poverty line: 35.8% (2011)

    70 cents earned per day, 58 cents per bag of rice, 120 cents on the black market

    3 million people in the Harat province had insufficient wheat
    Market price of food skyrocketed
    Rivers affected by drought (major one in 2012)
    Grueling winters = hypothermia

    CASE STUDY: M4 CORRIDOR


  • An industrial zone or factory


    • Runs from Wales to London

    Home to lots of tech firms like microelectronics, Rolls Royce, and British Aerospace because:
    - M4 motorway to transport inputs and outputs
    - Mainline railway Wales to London
    - Heathrow airport (and 4 others) for international links
    - Large labour force form London and nearby towns
    - Nearby firms to exchange ideas
    - Near Bristol, Bath, Reading, and London universities for expertise and research
    - Attractive environments for workers (e.g. national parks like Dartmoor)

    CASE STUDY: ECOTOURISM IN THE MONTEVERDE CLOUD FOREST


  • An area where tourism is important


    • Attractions:
    - Altitude of 1700m
    - 100 mammalian species, 2,500 plant species, >400 bird species, >120 reptile and amphibian species
    - Trails and trams, canopy tours, museums

    Advantages:
    - 80 new businesses opened since 1970s
    - The Reserva Biológica Bosque Nuboso Monteverde covers over 10,500 hectares
    - 400 full-time + 140 part-time jobs related to tourism

    Disadvantages:
    - Land prices have increased
    - People have migrated looking for jobs = pressure on local infrastructure
    - Atmosphere changed from a local Quaker community to a tourist centre
    - Tourists demanding more facilities and luxury
    - 40% of Monteverde’s amphibians have become extinct
    - Areas close to the trail = less wildlife
    - Some activities (e.g. ziplining) are not sustainable
    - Dirt track road = tourists arrive in 4x4 -> petrol + dust

    Management:
    - Budgets reserved to train + educate locals and tourists about protection of the reserve
    - Access to the cloud forest is strictly controlled

    CASE STUDY: ENERGY SUPPLY IN CHINA


  • Energy supply in a country or area


    • World’s largest emitter of greenhouse gases
    Industrial sector = 70% of energy use

    Coal: 3527Mt produced; 199Mt imported
    - Declining, peak in 2014 = 67% of energy consumption

    Petroleum: importing approximately 7 million barrels per day
    - Market dominated by Sinopec, CNPC, CNOOC
    - 18% of energy consumption in 2013

    Gas: 6% of energy consumption in 2013

    Hydro/Nuclear/Wind: 9% of energy consumption in 2013

    In 2013, 5000 coal mines were closed

    In 2016, China reached a total wind power generation capacity of 149GW
    - Generated 241 TWh of electricity

    In 2015, the hydropower capacity was 319GW
    - Generated 1,126 of energy (info about Three Gorges Dam relevant)

    CASE STUDY: WATER SUPPLY IN LESOTHO


  • Water supply in a country or area


    • The Lesotho Highlands Water Project is the largest civil engineering project in Africa

    When complete, it will divert 40% of Senqu fiver water through 5 large-scale dams

    After taking water for its own use, Lesotho will sell it to South Africa
    - Income will be used to develop its infrastructure and economy

    Lots of highlands which receive high rainfall
    - Valleys are ideal for building dams and reservoirs

    HEP can be generated with the dams

    Lakes will attract tourism, creating jobs and benefitting the economy
